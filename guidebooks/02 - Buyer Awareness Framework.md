## The Five Stages of Buyer Awareness

The single most important diagnostic question before writing a selling post:

> **Where is my reader in their journey?**

Every post must be calibrated to a specific awareness stage. Mismatching the post type to the reader's stage is one of the most common and costly mistakes in content marketing.

---

## Stage 1 — Unaware

**Who they are:** They don't know they have a problem. They aren't searching for solutions.

**Content goal:** Surface the problem through a story or relatable scenario.

**Best formats:**
- Story-led content (your own origin story, a pivotal moment)
- Relatable "before" scenarios
- Entertaining or curiosity-driven content

**Example angle:** *"I used to spend 4 hours every Monday doing something that now takes me 20 minutes..."*

> Do NOT pitch your product here. They aren't ready. The job is to create awareness of the problem itself.

---

## Stage 2 — Problem Aware

**Who they are:** They know they have a problem but don't know how to solve it.

**Content goal:** Educate them on the problem and introduce the idea that solutions exist.

**Best formats:**
- Educational breakdowns
- "Why this happens" explainers
- Tips and tutorials on the problem space

**Example angle:** *"Here are the 3 reasons your social media posts aren't getting clients..."*

---

## Stage 3 — Solution Aware

**Who they are:** They know solutions exist but haven't chosen one yet. They're comparing options.

**Content goal:** Show why your approach or offer is the best solution available.

**Best formats:**
- Comparison content ("Method A vs. Method B")
- Product or service demonstrations
- "Why I chose X over Y" type posts

**Example angle:** *"Most people try posting more to get more leads. Here's why that rarely works — and what does instead..."*

---

## Stage 4 — Product Aware

**Who they are:** They know about your offer but haven't committed yet. They need reassurance.

**Content goal:** Build trust through proof. Show that real people got real results.

**Best formats:**
- Case studies
- Client testimonials
- Before-and-after stories
- "Results my clients got" posts

**Example angle:** *"Sarah came to me with zero clients and left with a fully booked calendar in 6 weeks. Here's exactly what we did..."*

---

## Stage 5 — Most Aware

**Who they are:** They're ready to buy. They just need a reason to act *now*.

**Content goal:** Remove friction and trigger the decision with urgency or a direct offer.

**Best formats:**
- Hard-sale posts
- Urgency/scarcity announcements (limited spots, time-limited discount)
- Direct offer posts

**Example angle:** *"Enrollment closes Friday. Here's what's included and how to join before the deadline..."*

> This is the **only** stage where a hard CTA and direct pitch are appropriate.

---

## How to Use This Framework

### Step 1: Diagnose your audience
Where does the majority of your current audience sit on this scale?

### Step 2: Map your content calendar
Don't post only Stage 5 content. A healthy content mix might look like:

| Week | Stage Focus |
|------|-------------|
| Mon | Stage 1 (story-led, scroll-stopper) |
| Wed | Stage 2 (educational breakdown) |
| Fri | Stage 3 or 4 (comparison or case study) |
| Monthly | Stage 5 (direct offer, urgency post) |

### Step 3: Match the CTA to the stage
- Stages 1-3 → **Soft CTA**: "Follow for more", "Save this post", "Comment X for the guide"
- Stage 4 → **Trust CTA**: "Read more case studies at [link]", "DM me 'RESULTS'"
- Stage 5 → **Hard CTA**: "Click the link to book your spot now"

---

## The Story Inventory

At every stage, stories outperform facts. Build a **Story Inventory** — a library of personal stories you can draw from:

- **Origin story**: Why you started, what problem you personally solved
- **Pivotal moments**: Times your thinking or results dramatically changed
- **Client breakthrough stories**: Specific results with specific context
- **Lessons from failures**: What you got wrong and what it cost you
- **Childhood or early-career lessons**: Formative experiences that shaped your approach

> Stories stop the scroll. Fun facts don't. A reader remembers the story about your struggle. They forget the statistic you cited.
